Case Study 1: TiO2 Film on Borosilicate Glass

Rutile TiO2(110) surface — 11 atoms

GOOD CANDIDATE
Material Description

Titanium dioxide (TiO2) in the rutile crystal structure is the most studied thin-film oxide material. The rutile (110) surface is the lowest-energy facet and the most technologically relevant — it is the dominant surface in photocatalytic and photovoltaic applications.

The surface exposes rows of 5-fold coordinated Ti (Ti5c) sites alternating with rows of bridging oxygens (O_br). The Ti5c sites are electron-deficient (missing one oxygen ligand compared to the bulk octahedral coordination) and act as the primary adsorption sites.

Partial charges are from the Matsui–Akaogi empirical force field (1991), which is the standard for TiO2 surface simulation and gives Ti4+-like charges of +0.58 e for 5-fold surface Ti.

Key Findings
1
Dominant pair: O_NBO ↔ Ti5c

The non-bridging oxygen on glass and the 5-fold Ti site on TiO2 form the single strongest pair at −2.224 eV — 6.3× stronger than any other pair. The 1.90 Å separation maximises the 1/r³ term.

2
Ti5c is the primary film anchor

Seven of the top ten pairs involve a Ti5c site. The 6-fold Ti (Ti6c) never appears in the top 10. Under-coordinated surface Ti drives adhesion, consistent with DFT and STM studies of TiO2(110).

3
Surface hydroxyl enhances adhesion

The silanol hydrogen H_OH1 ranks 3rd and 4th, attracting both Ti5c (−0.188 eV) and a bridging oxygen (−0.171 eV). Humid glass surfaces are more adhesive to TiO2, not less.

Charges: Matsui, M. & Akaogi, M. (1991). Mol. Simul., 6(4), 239–244.
ITO

Case Study 2: ITO Film on Borosilicate Glass

In2O3 bixbyite (001) + 10% SnO2 — 11 atoms

EXCELLENT CANDIDATE
Material Description

Indium Tin Oxide (ITO) — In2O3 doped with approximately 10 mol% SnO2 — is the standard transparent conductive oxide (TCO) used as a front electrode in solar cells, flat-panel displays, and touchscreens. Its combination of high electrical conductivity (σ > 103 S/cm) and optical transparency (>80% in visible) is unmatched by any other commercial material.

The crystal structure is the bixbyite (Ia¯3) structure of In2O3. The (001) surface exposes 5-fold coordinated In (InO5) sites. Sn4+ substitutes In3+ at a 4-fold coordinated site, providing mobile electrons (carrier density ≈ 1021 cm-3).

Partial charges are from the Lewis-Catlow shell model as parameterised by Hamad, Moreira & Catlow (2011) for In2O3 surfaces.

Key Findings
1
ITO is 55% stronger than TiO2

The rank-1 pair O_NBO1 ↔ In5c1 reaches −3.44 eV compared to −2.22 eV for TiO2. In3+ has a larger local dipole moment (≈1.87 e·Å) than Ti4+ (≈1.50 e·Å), amplifying the interaction.

2
Sn dopant is a second strong anchor

The Sn4+ dopant (charge +0.950 e) ranks 2nd (−0.812 eV) despite being at slightly larger distance. The higher charge compensates for a smaller local dipole. This dual-anchor mechanism (In5c + Sn) makes ITO exceptionally well-pinned.

3
Favourable ratio of attractive pairs

54.5% attractive vs. 45.5% repulsive — an improvement over TiO2 (50.8% / 49.2%). ITO's lower oxygen density at z = 4.30 Å means fewer O–O repulsive pairs with the NBO row on glass.

Charges: Hamad, S., Moreira, N. H., & Catlow, C. R. A. (2011). J. Phys. Chem. C, 114(14), 2527–2535.
